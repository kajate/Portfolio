MARTA

Marta Fernández Guardado is a Spanish architect working and living in Berlin. She studied at Delft University of Technology in The Netherlands and Universidad Politécnica de Valencia in Spain, from where she graduated in 2010.

In 2012 she moved to Berlin to work for June14 Meyer-Grohbrügge & Chermayeff (http://june-14.com) and Sam Chermayeff Office (http://samchermayeffoffice.com) as a head designer for 6 years. During this period, while working in many different scale projects from landscape to furniture, she lead the design of the housing project in Kurfurstenstrasse 142-143 in Berlin, which is currently under construction by einsbisneun (http://einsbisneun-architekten.de) and whose design consultancy she still manages.

In 2017 she co-founded the furniture design studio PRETTY SOMETHING (http://www.prettysomething.de) with Diogo Passarinho and started to teach architectural design in Technische Universität Berlin in Germany.

In 2018 Marta joined the PhD design program PEP http://www.pep.tu-berlin.de and started an object-based exploration of the domestic, to reformulate her role as a architect while working on furniture scale artefacts.

PEP 0

The architectural construction is obviously a necessary requirement to create space, but it is not sufficient. Unlike territory, architecture needs relate to people in order to be complete; it needs to be inhabited. This binding process depends on the identification offered by the architecture and the actions and transformations that the inhabitants perform in it.

The phenomenon intensifies in housing architecture, which crisis in Europe still continues. Costs rise faster than incomes, particularly in major cities. Construction recovers slower than prices and shortage drives them up even more. Due to economical speculation, housing has become an investment objective economically “in use" even when houses are unoccupied. The market remains conservative and the housing model hardly evolves, identifying fewer and fewer people. Labour mobility, leisure long-term displacements and other migration flows are transferring our homes to not permanent and not owned, new platforms. Meaningful actions in the domestic space are discarded, especially among young renters. Objects (own, borrowed or shared) and ceremonies (daily, as a host or as a guest) become indispensable mechanisms to relate ourselves to the place we live in, and consequently part of the architecture.

Home is no more one and forever, but remains a place for protection, and even for presentation, reproduction and production. Its floor plan still represents a fragmented domesticity with specific rooms for every action, but not every action is always the same nor happens at the same time. Its furniture and equipment are used to program spaces, but not to program lives, which are constantly changing. The bed, for instance, traditionally one of the most intimate domestic inventions, is overtaking the sofa as the most used item of furniture and even becoming a regular working place.

The big and small architectural traditions that shape our lives are just assumptions that can be observed, questioned and redesigned. Meanwhile, in this context of crisis, mostly understood in economically terms, the capacity of radical exploration of architecture seems limited. Can the architect propose alternative explorations to the building? Can the architectural thinking apply to other scales and times? If space is created from the body, are there other active architectural bodies that may be used to relate people to foreign architectures, and even to other people and themselves? Can architectural ideas be tested through those bodies and be potentially applied to the architecture afterwards? Could they integrate emerging alternative domestic practises out of the dichotomy public/private, which architecture has not yet reached? The study proposes an object-based design investigation of the domestic environment as an architecture where things and bodies cohabit, in order to explore the architectural statement for contemporary ways of living.
